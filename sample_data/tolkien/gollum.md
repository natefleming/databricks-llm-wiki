# Gollum

Gollum, originally named Smeagol, is one of the most complex figures in [[middle-earth]]. A Hobbit-like creature corrupted over centuries by [[the-one-ring]], he serves as both guide and antagonist in The Lord of the Rings, and his role in the Ring's destruction is one of Tolkien's central themes about mercy and fate.

## Origins

Smeagol was a Stoor Hobbit who lived near the Gladden Fields around the year 2463 of the Third Age. When his cousin Deagol found [[the-one-ring]] in the river, Smeagol immediately desired it and murdered Deagol to possess it. This original act of violence set the pattern for his relationship with the Ring.

Shunned by his community for his increasingly strange behavior, Smeagol retreated to the caves beneath the Misty Mountains, where he lived for nearly 500 years. The Ring extended his life far beyond natural limits while twisting his body and mind. He became the creature known as Gollum, named for the gurgling noise in his throat.

## The Split Personality

Gollum's most distinctive trait is his divided self. "Smeagol" represents his original personality — capable of kindness, loneliness, and a desire for connection. "Gollum" represents the Ring's corruption — treacherous, violent, and obsessively possessive. These two sides argue openly with each other, a dramatic representation of [[the-one-ring]]'s psychological effects.

## Role in the Story

### Discovery by Bilbo
[[bilbo-baggins]] encountered Gollum in the caves of the Misty Mountains and won [[the-one-ring]] through a riddle contest. Bilbo's act of mercy — choosing not to kill the wretched creature — proved to be the pivotal moral decision of the entire legendarium.

### Guide to Mordor
Captured by [[frodo-baggins]] and [[samwise-gamgee]], Gollum swore an oath on the Ring to serve the "Master of the Precious." He guided them through the Dead Marshes and toward [[mordor]]. During this time, the Smeagol personality briefly gained dominance, and he showed genuine affection toward Frodo.

However, Gollum ultimately led the Hobbits into Shelob's lair as a trap, hoping the spider would kill them so he could reclaim the Ring.

### The Destruction of the Ring

At the Crack of Doom inside [[mount-doom]], when [[frodo-baggins]] claimed [[the-one-ring]] and refused to destroy it, Gollum attacked him, bit off his finger, and seized the Ring. In his ecstatic triumph, he danced on the edge and fell into the volcanic fire, destroying both himself and the Ring.

This moment — the Ring's destruction through Gollum's obsession rather than anyone's deliberate choice — is central to Tolkien's themes. [[gandalf]]'s earlier words to Frodo proved prophetic: "The pity of Bilbo may rule the fate of many."

## Thematic Significance

Gollum represents the ultimate effect of [[the-one-ring]] on its bearers. He shows what [[bilbo-baggins]] might have become given enough centuries, and what [[frodo-baggins]] was slowly becoming. His existence serves as both warning and, paradoxically, salvation — without Gollum, the Ring could never have been destroyed, for no one could willingly cast it into the fire.

Tolkien uses Gollum to argue that even the most wretched and corrupted beings have a role to play, and that mercy toward them — even when it seems foolish — may prove to be the wisest choice of all.
