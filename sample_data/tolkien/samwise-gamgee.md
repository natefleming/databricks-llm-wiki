# Samwise Gamgee

Samwise "Sam" Gamgee is the loyal companion of [[frodo-baggins]] throughout The Lord of the Rings and is widely regarded as the true hero of the story. A gardener from [[the-shire]], Sam accompanied Frodo on his quest to destroy [[the-one-ring]] in [[mount-doom]].

## Background

Sam was the son of Hamfast "the Gaffer" Gamgee, gardener at Bag End for [[bilbo-baggins]] and later [[frodo-baggins]]. He grew up tending the gardens of Bag End and listening to Bilbo's stories of Elves and adventures, which kindled in him an unusual (for a Hobbit) love of Elvish lore and a desire to see the wider world.

Sam was recruited into the quest when [[gandalf]] caught him eavesdropping on his conversation with Frodo about [[the-one-ring]]. Gandalf assigned him as Frodo's companion, recognizing that Sam's simple loyalty and devotion would be the Ring-bearer's greatest protection.

## The Journey

Sam proved indispensable throughout the quest. After the Breaking of the Fellowship, he was the only companion to continue with [[frodo-baggins]] into [[mordor]]. He carried supplies, cooked meals, maintained morale, and physically carried Frodo up the slopes of [[mount-doom]] when the Ring-bearer could no longer walk.

Sam briefly bore [[the-one-ring]] himself when he believed Frodo had been killed by Shelob. Notably, the Ring had less hold over Sam than over almost any other character — his humble desires (a garden, home, and family) gave the Ring little to amplify or corrupt.

## After the War

Sam returned to [[the-shire]], married Rosie Cotton, and became one of the most important Hobbits in Shire history. He served as Mayor of the Shire for seven consecutive terms (49 years). He used the gift of soil from Galadriel of [[lothlorien]] to replant the Shire after its scouring.

After Rosie's death, Sam was said to have sailed to the Undying Lands — the last of the Ring-bearers to depart [[middle-earth]].

## Significance

Tolkien himself stated that Sam was the "chief hero" of The Lord of the Rings. While [[frodo-baggins]] bore the Ring, Sam bore Frodo. His unwavering loyalty, practical wisdom, and refusal to despair even in the darkest moments of [[mordor]] embody Tolkien's belief that ordinary courage — the courage of those who have no power and no expectation of glory — is the most admirable form of heroism.
